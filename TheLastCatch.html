Overview

"The Last Catch" is an innovative puzzle game that combines the strategic elements of Minesweeper with a compelling underwater narrative. Set in a future where the oceans have become perilous due to scattered underwater mines, players take on the role of a skilled diver tasked with clearing the seas to restore marine ecosystems.

Navigate through a grid-based ocean floor, using your wit and specialized equipment to detect and mark hazardous areas while uncovering safe zones teeming with marine life. Each successfully cleared area brings you one step closer to reviving the ocean and discovering the truth behind the environmental catastrophe.

My Role

In the early stages of development, I experimented with the mechanics and framework of the game. Initially, I built a prototype that felt too similar to existing fishing games, so I shifted the focus from traditional gear upgrades to a more abstract, yet engaging approach inspired by the mechanics of Minesweeper. By introducing "world events," such as oil spills and algae blooms, I created a dynamic game environment that balanced entertainment with an educational message. These events served to emphasize the importance of sustainable fishing practices and how environmental factors can impact marine ecosystems.

Feedback was an integral part of my development process. I regularly tested the game with peers and external audiences, incorporating their insights to improve usability, gameplay balance, and overall coherence. Some of the key changes included adjusting the game’s difficulty curve, refining the user interface, and adding visual effects that improved player feedback. For example, I changed the way fish spawned from the tiles to provide clearer connections between player actions and game outcomes, making the gameplay feel smoother and more intuitive.

To make the game more engaging, I also explored different art styles and incorporated AI-generated assets, which I later refined to match the game’s theme. This allowed me to create visually appealing elements without sacrificing the project’s timeline. Throughout development, I also focused on adding pop-up dialogues to introduce different perspectives on overfishing, such as those from environmentalists, government officials, and workers. This provided players with multiple viewpoints and encouraged reflection on the trade-offs of different solutions to overfishing.

As the game evolved, I continued to polish and refine various features, particularly focusing on making the gameplay more interactive and accessible. I incorporated feedback from playtests to improve aspects like the game timer, tutorial design, and difficulty progression, ensuring that the learning experience was as smooth and engaging as possible. Additionally, I experimented with mechanics such as rogue fishing boats and real-world events like illegal fishing to make the game more dynamic and reflective of real-life challenges in the fishing industry.
